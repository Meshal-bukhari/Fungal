Tinea Versicolor (Pityriasis Versicolor)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Hypo- or hyperpigmented macules/patches, fine scale (may need scratching to see), often confluent.
  • Distribution: Upper trunk, shoulders (seborrheic areas), neck, face (children). "Inverse" in flexural areas.
  • Risk Factors: Heat, humidity, oily skin, hyperhidrosis, immunosuppression.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: "Spaghetti and meatballs" - yeast and short hyphae on KOH prep. More accurately "ziti and meatballs".
  • Special Stains: PAS highlights yeast and hyphae in stratum corneum.
  • Culture: Requires oil overlay (lipophilic yeast), generally not needed.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical): Selenium sulfide shampoo, ketoconazole shampoo, ciclopirox shampoo, zinc pyrithione shampoo (body washes). Imidazole creams (ketoconazole, clotrimazole), ciclopirox cream/gel/lotion (for localized areas).
  • Drug Selection (Oral - for extensive disease/failed topical): Fluconazole (200mg daily x 5-7 days, or weekly x 2-3 weeks, or 400mg single dose), Itraconazole (200mg daily x 5-7 days). Avoid oral ketoconazole due to risks. Terbinafine and Griseofulvin not good options
  • Dosing & Duration: Topical shampoos/creams typically 2-4 weeks. Oral regimens are short courses.
  • Maintenance Therapy: Crucial for recurrence prevention. Topical imidazole daily, antifungal shampoo 1-2 times weekly.

Specific Fungi:

Malassezia furfur, Malassezia globosa.

Unique Identifiers:

Asymptomatic, cosmetic concern, KOH "spaghetti and meatballs", recurrence common.

Tinea Nigra

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Solitary, sharply demarcated, brown to gray-green macule or patch, velvety or mild scale. Resembles acral melanocytic nevus but larger, lighter, no linear striations.
  • Distribution: Palms (most common), soles, neck, trunk.
  • Risk Factors: Tropical climates.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Septate pigmented hyphae on KOH prep.
  • Special Stains: Pigmented hyphae visible without special stains.
  • Culture: Hortaea werneckii - slow-growing, green to black colony.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical): Keratolytics (Whitfield ointment), azoles, allylamines.
  • Dosing & Duration: Several weeks to prevent recurrence.
  • Systemic Therapy: Not indicated.

Specific Fungi:

Hortaea werneckii (formerly Exophiala werneckii).

Unique Identifiers:

Dark macule on palm/sole, KOH pigmented hyphae, tropical locations.

Piedra (Black Piedra & White Piedra)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Black Piedra): Asymptomatic, hard, brown to black nodules firmly attached to hair shaft.
  • Appearance (White Piedra): Soft, less adherent nodules, white to light brown/red/green on hair shaft.
  • Distribution: Hair shaft (scalp - black, beard/pubic - white).
  • Risk Factors: Tropical climates, poor hygiene.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy (Black Piedra): Dematiaceous hyphae, asci with 8 ascospores in nodule crush prep.
  • Microscopy (White Piedra): Non-dematiaceous hyphae, blastoconidia, arthroconidia in nodule crush prep.
  • Culture (Black Piedra): Piedraia hortae - slow, green-black, velvety colony.
  • Culture (White Piedra): Trichosporon spp. - rapid, moist, cream-colored, yeast-like colony. Inhibited by cycloheximide.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical): 2% Ketoconazole shampoo. Oral terbinafine may be considered.
  • Mechanical: Clipping affected hair is crucial.
  • Dosing & Duration: Shampoo use until nodules clear, clipping as needed.

Specific Fungi:

Piedraia hortae (Black), Trichosporon spp. (White).

Unique Identifiers:

Nodules on hair shaft, KOH/microscopy, hair clipping treatment.

Tinea Capitis (Scalp Ringworm)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Variable: scaling, patchy alopecia, "black dots" (broken hairs), kerion (inflammatory boggy plaque), pustules.
  • Distribution: Scalp, hair shafts.
  • Risk Factors: Children, household contact, contaminated fomites, Down syndrome.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyphae and arthroconidia within or around hair shafts on KOH prep. Endothrix vs. ectothrix patterns.
  • Special Stains: Chlorazol black stain enhances hyphae.
  • Wood's Lamp: fluoresce (Microsporum - ectothrix) Microsporum canis (greenish fluorescence) Microsporum audouinii (blue-green fluorescence), bluish-white fluorescence (T. schoenleinii). T. tonsurans (endothrix) does not fluoresce.
  • Culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar, dermatophyte test medium (DTM).

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Oral - required for scalp infection): Griseofulvin (microsize or ultramicrosize), Terbinafine (children > 4 years and >20kg), Itraconazole (pulse or continuous), Fluconazole.
  • Topical Adjuncts: Antifungal shampoos (selenium sulfide, ketoconazole, ciclopirox) to reduce shedding and transmission, but not curative alone.
  • Dosing & Duration: Griseofulvin 6-8 weeks For microsize formulation: Children: 10-20 mg/kg/day in single or divided doses ultramicrosize formulation (which has better bioavailability): Children: 5-10 mg/kg/day in single or divided doses, Terbinafine 3-4 weeks, Itraconazole 4-8 weeks, Fluconazole 3-6 weeks. Duration depends on clinical response.
  • Treatment Modifications: Kerion - may consider oral steroids to reduce inflammation and scarring for 7-10 days starting at 0.5 mg/kg/day and tapering to 0.. Household contact screening and treatment with shampoos.

Specific Fungi:

Trichophyton tonsurans (endothrix, black dot, most common in US), Microsporum canis (ectothrix, gray patch, fluoresces), Trichophyton violaceum (endothrix, black dot).

Unique Identifiers:

Pediatric age group, scalp involvement, alopecia, kerion, oral treatment required, endothrix vs ectothrix patterns.

Tinea Corporis (Body Ringworm)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Annular plaques with raised, scaly, erythematous border and central clearing. May be arcuate, circinate, concentric, oval, or figurate. Vesicles/pustules in active border suggest tinea. "Tinea incognito" - modified presentation due to topical steroid use (less scale, less raised border, more subtle).The lesions may be extensive or even vegetating, and a prolonged granulomatous reaction (as described by Majocchi) can develop.
  • Distribution: Trunk, extremities (excluding scalp, nails, palms, soles, groin). Often exposed skin.
  • Risk Factors: Contact with infected humans/animals, contaminated fomites, military housing, gyms, wrestling, outdoor activities.
  • Organisms: trichophyton Rubrum: Most common cause tinea corporis and Majochi granuloma. trichophyton concetricum: causative organism of imbricata. M. gypseum with bullous lesions

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyaline, septate hyphae in stratum corneum on KOH prep.
  • Special Stains: Chlorazol black stain enhances hyphae. PAS stain on skin biopsy.
  • Culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar, dermatophyte test medium (DTM).

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical - for localized disease): Azole creams (ketoconazole, clotrimazole, miconazole), allylamine creams (terbinafine, naftifine), ciclopirox cream/gel/lotion.
  • Drug Selection (Oral - for extensive, inflammatory, or failed topical): Terbinafine, Itraconazole, Fluconazole. Griseofulvin is 2nd line.
  • Dosing & Duration: Topical 2-4 weeks. Oral Terbinafine 1-2 weeks, Itraconazole 1-2 weeks, Fluconazole 2-4 weeks. Duration depends on clinical clearance.
  • Treatment Modifications: "Tinea incognito" - stop topical steroid, treat as tinea corporis.

Specific Fungi:

Trichophyton rubrum (most common worldwide), Trichophyton mentagrophytes, Microsporum canis (zoonotic).

Unique Identifiers:

Annular lesions with central clearing, scaly raised border, KOH hyphae, topical or oral treatment depending on extent.

Tinea Cruris (Jock Itch)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Pruritic, sharply demarcated, erythematous, scaly plaques in inguinal folds and upper inner thighs. Raised, erythematous, scaly advancing border, may have vesicles/pustules. Scrotum usually spared. Can be unilateral or bilateral. Chronic infections may become leathery and lichenified.
  • Distribution: Inguinal folds, upper inner thighs, crural folds. May extend to abdomen and buttocks. Spares the scrotum ( Vs Candidiasis which involved scrotum)
  • Risk Factors: Male sex, warm/moist environment (scrotum), obesity, hyperhidrosis, tinea pedis, occlusive clothing.
  • Organisms: Tinea Rubrum: Most common cause of tinea cruris , usually chronic. Epidermophyton floccosum: Common in locker room epidemics and causative agent of eczema marginatum. Trichophyton mentagrophytes: More acute and severe infections, may rapidly spread to trunk and lower extremities.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyaline, septate hyphae in stratum corneum on KOH prep.
  • Special Stains: Chlorazol black stain enhances hyphae. PAS stain on skin biopsy.
  • Culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar, dermatophyte test medium (DTM).

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical): Azole creams (ketoconazole, clotrimazole, econazole), allylamine creams (terbinafine, naftifine), ciclopirox cream/gel/lotion.
  • Drug Selection (Oral - for extensive, inflammatory, or failed topical): Terbinafine, Itraconazole, Fluconazole. Griseofulvin 2nd line.
  • Dosing & Duration: Topical 2-4 weeks. Oral Terbinafine 1-2 weeks, Itraconazole 1-2 weeks, Fluconazole 1-2 weeks. Duration depends on clinical clearance.
  • Treatment Modifications: Hygiene measures (loose clothing, drying thoroughly), treat concomitant tinea pedis.

Specific Fungi:

Epidermophyton floccosum, Trichophyton rubrum, Trichophyton mentagrophytes.

Unique Identifiers:

Inguinal folds, sharply demarcated, pruritic, KOH hyphae, rule out candidiasis if scrotum involved or erosions/satellite pustules are present.

Tinea Pedis (Athlete's Foot)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Moccasin type): Diffuse hyperkeratosis, erythema, scaling, fissures on soles and lateral aspects of feet. Chronic, dry.
  • Appearance (Interdigital type): Erythema, scaling, fissures, maceration in web spaces, especially 4th and 5th. "Dermatophytosis complex" - bacterial superinfection.
  • Appearance (Vesicular/Inflammatory type): Vesicles and bullae, often on medial foot, inflammatory. Dermatophytid reaction possible.
  • Appearance (Ulcerative type): Ulcers and erosions in web spaces, often 4th web space, secondary bacterial infection common, seen in immunocompromised/diabetics.
  • Distribution: Soles, lateral feet, interdigital web spaces. May extend to dorsum.
  • Risk Factors: Occlusive footwear, communal showers, hyperhidrosis, diabetes, immunocompromise.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyaline, septate hyphae in stratum corneum on KOH prep.
  • Special Stains: Chlorazol black stain enhances hyphae. PAS stain on skin biopsy.
  • Culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar, dermatophyte test medium (DTM).

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical - most types): Azole creams (clotrimazole, miconazole, econazole), allylamine creams (terbinafine, naftifine), ciclopirox cream/gel/lotion. For moccasin type, consider agents with urea or lactic acid to aid penetration.
  • Drug Selection (Oral - for moccasin type, inflammatory, nail involvement, failed topical, diabetics, immunocompromised): Terbinafine, Itraconazole, Fluconazole. Griseofulvin is 2nds line.
  • Dosing & Duration: Topical 4-6 weeks, often longer for moccasin type. Oral Terbinafine 2 weeks, Itraconazole 1 week (pulse or continuous), Fluconazole 2-4 weeks. Onychomycosis (if present) requires longer oral duration.
  • Treatment Modifications: Interdigital type with bacterial superinfection - consider topical or oral antibiotics. Vesicular/inflammatory type - topical antifungal usually sufficient, but consider oral if severe dermatophytid reaction. Hygiene measures (dry feet, breathable footwear, antifungal powders).

Specific Fungi:

Trichophyton rubrum, Trichophyton interdigitale (formerly T. mentagrophytes var. interdigitale), Epidermophyton floccosum.

Unique Identifiers:

Foot involvement, various clinical patterns (moccasin, interdigital, vesicular, ulcerative), KOH hyphae, hygiene crucial for prevention, consider bacterial superinfection.

Tinea Manuum (Hand Ringworm)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Dorsal Hand - Tinea Corporis-like): Annular plaque, scaly border, central clearing.
  • Appearance (Palmar/Digital - Tinea Manuum proper): Unilateral (often "one hand, two feet syndrome"), hyperkeratosis of palms and digits (especially creases), scaling, may be exfoliative, vesicular, or papular. Often chronic.
  • Distribution: Dorsal hand, palms, digits, interdigital spaces of hand.
  • Risk Factors: Often associated with tinea pedis, exposure to dermatophytes.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyaline, septate hyphae in stratum corneum on KOH prep.
  • Special Stains: Chlorazol black stain enhances hyphae. PAS stain on skin biopsy.
  • Culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar, dermatophyte test medium (DTM).

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical - Dorsal Hand type, may be sufficient): Azole creams, allylamine creams, ciclopirox cream/gel/lotion.
  • Drug Selection (Oral - Palmar/Digital type, often required due to hyperkeratosis): Terbinafine, Itraconazole, Fluconazole. Griseofulvin is not effective.
  • Dosing & Duration: Topical 4-6 weeks (dorsal hand). Oral Terbinafine 2-4 weeks, Itraconazole 2-4 weeks, Fluconazole 2-4 weeks (palmar/digital). Duration depends on clinical clearance and onychomycosis treatment if present.
  • Treatment Modifications: Keratolytics (urea cream, lactic acid) as adjuncts to topical or oral antifungals for palmar/digital type to reduce hyperkeratosis. Treat associated tinea pedis and onychomycosis.

Specific Fungi:

Trichophyton rubrum, Trichophyton mentagrophytes, Epidermophyton floccosum.

Unique Identifiers:

Hand involvement, unilateral palmar hyperkeratosis ("one hand, two feet syndrome"), KOH hyphae, oral treatment often needed for palmar type.

Tinea Faciei (Face Ringworm)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Variable presentation - some cases have classic dermatophyte features (scale, annular configuration, pustules at border), but often presents with subtle erythema, scale, or hyperpigmented lesions with minimal annular features.
  • Distribution: Face (specific areas include perinasal, perioral, cheeks), sparing the beard area (which would be classified as Tinea Barbae).
  • Risk Factors: Similar to Tinea Corporis - exposure to infected individuals, animals, or fomites.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyaline, septate hyphae in stratum corneum on KOH prep (similar to Tinea Corporis).
  • Special Stains: Chlorazol black stain enhances hyphae, PAS stain on skin biopsy.
  • Culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar, dermatophyte test medium (DTM).
  • Diagnostic Challenge: Requires high index of suspicion due to variable and subtle presentations.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical): Azole creams (ketoconazole, clotrimazole, miconazole), allylamine creams (terbinafine, naftifine), similar to Tinea Corporis.
  • Drug Selection (Oral): For extensive or resistant cases - terbinafine, itraconazole, fluconazole.
  • Dosing & Duration: Topical 2-4 weeks, oral antifungals as per Tinea Corporis protocols.
  • Treatment Modifications: Care with facial applications, avoid steroid combinations which can lead to Tinea Incognito.

Specific Fungi:

Same as Tinea Corporis: Trichophyton rubrum, Trichophyton mentagrophytes, Microsporum canis (zoonotic).

Unique Identifiers:

Often misdiagnosed as other facial dermatoses (seborrheic dermatitis, perioral dermatitis, contact dermatitis, rosacea, lupus erythematosus, acne vulgaris, annular psoriasis). Tinea Incognito occurs when prior topical corticosteroid use masks typical features. Lack of classic "ringworm" appearance makes diagnosis challenging. Consider when presumed facial dermatoses don't respond to appropriate treatments.

Tinea Barbae (Beard Ringworm)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Inflammatory): Deep, inflammatory, follicular pustules, nodules, plaques in beard area. May have kerion-like boggy plaques, abscesses, sinus tracts, bacterial superinfection, scarring alopecia, constitutional symptoms (malaise, lymphadenopathy). Zoophilic organisms more likely to cause inflammatory type.
  • Appearance (Superficial): Less inflammatory, tinea corporis-like plaques with scale, central alopecia (reversible). Anthropophilic organisms more likely to cause superficial type.
  • Distribution: Bearded areas of face and neck.
  • Risk Factors: Exposure to animals (zoophilic), barbershop exposure (less common now), compromised skin barrier.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyphae and arthroconidia in hair shafts and follicles on KOH prep.
  • Special Stains: Chlorazol black stain enhances hyphae. PAS stain on biopsy (kerion).
  • Culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar, dermatophyte test medium (DTM). Culture is important to identify organism and guide treatment, especially in inflammatory type.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Oral - required, especially for inflammatory type): Terbinafine, Itraconazole, Fluconazole. Griseofulvin may be used but less effective, especially for zoophilic infections.
  • Topical Adjuncts: Topical antifungals not sufficient as monotherapy. Warm compresses for kerion.
  • Dosing & Duration: Oral Terbinafine 2-4 weeks, Itraconazole 2-4 weeks, Fluconazole 3-4 weeks. Duration longer for kerion and deep infections, may be months.
  • Treatment Modifications: For kerion, consider oral steroids to reduce inflammation and scarring. Bacterial superinfection - treat with antibiotics. Shaving may be discouraged initially in inflammatory type.

Specific Fungi:

Trichophyton verrucosum, Trichophyton mentagrophytes (zoophilic, inflammatory). Trichophyton rubrum (anthropophilic, superficial). Microsporum canis (less common).

Unique Identifiers:

Beard area, inflammatory pustules/nodules/plaques (especially zoophilic), kerion, oral treatment required, differentiate from bacterial folliculitis, rule out sinus tract/dental origin.

Tinea Unguium (Onychomycosis)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Distal/Lateral Subungual - DLSO, most common): Onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis, yellowing/thickening, nail plate crumbling, starts distally and laterally.
  • Appearance (Superficial White - SWO): White, chalky patches on dorsal nail plate surface.
  • Appearance (Proximal Subungual - PSO): Proximal nail plate involvement, often with paronychia, often in immunocompromised.
  • Appearance (Total Dystrophic - TDO): Entire nail plate thickened, dystrophic, crumbling, end-stage of other patterns.
  • Distribution: Toenails (more common), fingernails. Often multiple nails involved.
  • Risk Factors: Age, trauma, tinea pedis, reduced peripheral circulation, diabetes, hyperhidrosis, onychogryphosis, genetic predisposition (Down syndrome).
  • Indicators of Severe Onychomycosis & Poor Treatment Response:
    • Nail Factors: Subungual hyperkeratosis >2 mm, significant lateral involvement, >50% nail bed involvement.Slow nail growth, total dystrophic onychomycosis, and matrix involvement predict poor response
    • Dermatophytoma: Streak/patch of densely packed hyphae â€“ removal may improve antifungal efficacy.
    • Patient Factors: Immunosuppression, peripheral arterial disease, and poorly controlled diabetes increase severity and recurrence risk.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyaline, septate hyphae and arthroconidia in nail clippings/subungual debris on KOH prep. Relatively low sensitivity.
  • Special Stains: PAS stain on nail clipping histology - gold standard for diagnosis, high sensitivity.
  • Culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar, dermatophyte test medium (DTM). Lower sensitivity than PAS, but identifies organism.
  • Nail Biopsy: For histology and PAS, consider if diagnosis unclear or to rule out other nail pathology.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical - for mild DLSO, SWO, limited nail involvement): Ciclopirox 8% solution, Tavaborole 5% solution, Efinaconazole 10% solution. Limited efficacy for most onychomycosis.
  • Drug Selection (Oral - for moderate to severe DLSO, PSO, TDO, multiple nail involvement, failed topical): Terbinafine (gold standard, best efficacy for dermatophytes), Itraconazole (pulse or continuous), Fluconazole (less effective than terbinafine/itraconazole for dermatophytes). Oral Griseofulvin is not effective.
  • Dosing & Duration (Oral): Terbinafine 12 weeks toenails, 6 weeks fingernails. Itraconazole continuous 12 weeks toenails, 6 weeks fingernails, or pulse dosing. Fluconazole continuous until clear (longer duration).
  • Treatment Modifications: Debridement of hyperkeratotic nail may improve topical penetration. Consider combination therapy (oral + topical) for severe cases or poor responders. Monitor LFTs with oral antifungals. Counsel on long treatment duration and recurrence risk.

Specific Fungi:

Trichophyton rubrum (most common), Trichophyton interdigitale, Epidermophyton floccosum. Non-dermatophyte molds (Fusarium, Scopulariopsis) and Candida can also cause onychomycosis, but less common in healthy individuals.Fusarium spp.: Causes superficial white onychomycosis but is of particular concern in immunocompromised patients due to its ability to cause disseminated fusariosis. It is highly resistant to antifungals, often requiring amphotericin B or voriconazole. Scytalidium spp.: Associated with dark onychomycosis and often linked to chronic paronychia. It is resistant to azoles and terbinafine, making treatment challenging. Scopulariopsis brevicaulis: Causes white superficial onychomycosis with lateral yellow-brown discoloration. KOH prep reveals lemon-shaped conidia and atypical hyphae.Candida spp.: Common in chronic mucocutaneous candidiasis, frequently affecting fingernails, leading to ridging, yellow discoloration, and onycholysis. More common in children under 3 years and patients with chronic paronychia.

Unique Identifiers:

Nail involvement (toenails > fingernails), various clinical patterns (DLSO, SWO, PSO, TDO), KOH/PAS/culture for diagnosis, oral treatment often required, long treatment duration, recurrence common.

Malassezia (Pityrosporum) Folliculitis

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Pruritic, monomorphic, follicular papules and pustules. No comedones.
  • Distribution: Upper trunk, arms, neck, face (forehead, hairline). Sebum-rich areas.
  • Risk Factors: Young adults, warm/humid climate, hyperhidrosis, oily skin, immunosuppression, chronic antibiotic use, topical oil application, occlusion.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Yeast forms only (no hyphae) in follicular contents or skin scraping KOH prep.
  • Special Stains: PAS stain highlights yeast in hair follicle.
  • Culture: Requires lipid-enriched media (e.g., Sabouraud dextrose agar with olive oil overlay). Culture generally not needed for diagnosis.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Topical): Selenium sulfide shampoo (body wash), ketoconazole shampoo (body wash), ciclopirox shampoo (body wash), zinc pyrithione shampoo (body wash). Topical azole creams (ketoconazole, clotrimazole), ciclopirox cream/gel/lotion. Benzoyl peroxide wash may also be helpful (reduces follicular occlusion).
  • Drug Selection (Oral - for extensive, recalcitrant, or severe cases): Itraconazole, Fluconazole.
  • Dosing & Duration: Topical washes daily or several times weekly for 2-4 weeks. Topical creams twice daily for 2-4 weeks. Oral Itraconazole or Fluconazole short courses (e.g., 200mg daily x 1-2 weeks).
  • Treatment Modifications: Address underlying risk factors (reduce occlusion, switch antibiotics if possible, avoid oily products).

Specific Fungi:

Malassezia furfur, Malassezia globosa.

Unique Identifiers:

Pruritic follicular papules/pustules without comedones, upper trunk/face, yeast forms only on KOH, responds to antifungals. Differentiate from bacterial folliculitis and acne vulgaris.

Sporotrichosis (Rose Gardener's Disease)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Lymphocutaneous - Sporotrichoid, most common): Initial papule/nodule at inoculation site (often hand/arm), becomes ulcerated. Secondary nodules/ulcers along lymphatic drainage pathway proximally. "Ascending lymphangitis with nodules".
  • Appearance (Fixed Cutaneous): Localized verrucous plaque or ulcer at inoculation site, no lymphatic spread. Often face (especially children).
  • Appearance (Disseminated): Subcutaneous nodules, systemic involvement possible (lungs, bones, joints, CNS - rare). Immunocompromised hosts at higher risk.
  • Distribution: Extremities (lymphocutaneous), face (fixed cutaneous), widespread (disseminated).
  • Risk Factors: Gardening, landscaping, forestry, contact with sphagnum moss, rose thorns, cats (especially S. brasiliensis). Endemic areas (Americas, Africa).

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Organisms sparse and difficult to see on routine stains (H&E, KOH). Cigar-shaped yeast forms. Asteroid bodies (radiating eosinophilic material around yeast) may be seen but not specific.
  • Special Stains: PAS and GMS enhance yeast. Direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) staining increases sensitivity.
  • Culture: Gold standard for diagnosis. Dimorphic fungus. Mold form at 25Â°C (white to brown wrinkled colony), yeast form at 37Â°C (pasty, cream-colored).

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Oral - Lymphocutaneous, Fixed Cutaneous): Itraconazole (first-line), Saturated Solution of Potassium Iodide (SSKI - older, less preferred due to side effects).
  • Drug Selection (IV - Disseminated, Severe): Amphotericin B (deoxycholate or lipid formulation), followed by oral Itraconazole. Voriconazole, Posaconazole, Isavuconazole are alternatives.
  • Dosing & Duration (Oral): Itraconazole 100-200mg daily for 3-6 months, or until resolution and consolidation. SSKI titrated up to tolerance, continued for several weeks post-resolution.
  • Dosing & Duration (IV): Amphotericin B until clinical improvement, then step-down to oral azole.
  • Treatment Modifications: Localized cutaneous lesions - may consider local heat therapy in addition to antifungals. Disseminated disease - prolonged therapy, consider underlying immunosuppression.

Specific Fungi:

Sporothrix schenckii, Sporothrix brasiliensis, Sporothrix globosa, Sporothrix mexicana. S. brasiliensis - cat-associated, more virulent.

Unique Identifiers:

Lymphocutaneous spread, "rose gardener's disease", cigar-shaped yeasts, asteroid bodies, dimorphic fungus, culture for definitive diagnosis, Itraconazole treatment. Differentiate from atypical mycobacterial infection (especially M. marinum).

Chromoblastomycosis (Verrucous Dermatitis)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Verrucous plaques, nodules, papules, often with cauliflower-like appearance. May have pigmented dots (reddish-brown to black). Chronic, slowly progressive.
  • Distribution: Lower extremities (most common - foot, leg), exposed areas prone to trauma. Unilateral usually.
  • Risk Factors: Tropical/subtropical climates, outdoor occupations (farmers, laborers), trauma, lack of footwear.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Sclerotic bodies (Medlar bodies, copper pennies) - pathognomonic, thick-walled, pigmented cells, seen in tissue (dermis, giant cells).
  • Special Stains: Fontana-Masson staining for melanin helps confirm the presence of the sclerotic bodies.
  • Culture: Slow-growing, dematiaceous (pigmented) colonies. Fonsecaea pedrosoi (most common), Cladophialophora carrionii .

Treatment Specifics:

+

Clinical Presentation:

Basidiobolus ranarum.

Painless subcutaneous swelling, broad non-septate hyphae with eosinophilic cuff (Splendore-Hoeppli), SSKI treatment of choice, children, tropical climates, bat/arthropod exposure. +

Clinical Presentation:

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Cutaneous - Primary): Necrotic cellulitis, plaques, ulcers, bullae, eschar. Rapidly progressive. Often at trauma site.
  • Appearance (Cutaneous - Secondary/Contiguous spread from sinuses): Facial edema (unilateral), black eschar on palate or nasal mucosa, periorbital cellulitis, proptosis, cranial nerve palsies (Rhino-orbital-cerebral mucormycosis - most common form).
  • Appearance (Pulmonary): Fever, cough, hemoptysis, pleuritic chest pain.
  • Appearance (Disseminated): Rhino-orbital-cerebral, pulmonary, cutaneous, gastrointestinal, disseminated.
  • Distribution: Ubiquitous in environment (soil, decaying matter), sinuses (rhino-orbital-cerebral), lungs, skin (trauma site).
  • Risk Factors: DM/DKA Diabetes Mellitus (especially DKA) - classic risk factor, Neutropenia (less common than Aspergillosis, but still a risk), Immunosuppression (steroids, transplant), Iron overload (deferoxamine use), trauma/burns.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Broad, ribbon-like, non-septate hyphae with 90-degree branching in tissue (biopsy - sinus, skin, lung). Angioinvasion - prominent.
  • Special Stains: H&E, PAS, Methenamine silver highlight hyphae, but poor staining with H&E can be characteristic.
  • Culture: Rapidly growing, "lid-lifter" colonies on Sabouraud dextrose agar, fluffy white-gray colonies. Tissue biopsy culture.
  • No reliable serologic or antigen tests. PCR-based assays developing but not widely available.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (First-line): Lipid Amphotericin B (deoxycholate or lipid formulation) - high dose, Posaconazole or Isavuconazole (oral/IV - step-down or alternative to ampho B for less severe).
  • Drug Selection (Salvage/Combination): Echinocandins (no activity against Mucorales as monotherapy). Combination Amphotericin B + Posaconazole/Isavuconazole or other agents sometimes used.
  • Non-Pharmacologic: Aggressive surgical debridement of necrotic tissue - cornerstone of therapy. Reverse underlying risk factors (DKA management, neutropenia reversal). Hyperbaric oxygen (adjunct).
  • Dosing & Duration: Amphotericin B and Posaconazole/Isavuconazole high doses and prolonged duration, guided by clinical response, often weeks to months.

Specific Fungi:

Rhizopus, Mucor, Lichtheimia (formerly Absidia), Rhizomucor. Rhizopus - most common genus.

Unique Identifiers:

Non-septate hyphae with 90-degree branching, angioinvasion, necrosis, rhino-orbital-cerebral presentation, diabetes (DKA) - classic risk, aggressive surgical debridement, Lipid Amphotericin B/Posaconazole/Isavuconazole treatment. Differentiate from Aspergillosis (septate hyphae), bacterial cellulitis, necrotizing fasciitis. Emerging in association with COVID-19.

Cryptococcosis

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Cutaneous - Disseminated): Papules, nodules, pustules, plaques, ulcers, cellulitis, molluscum contagiosum-like lesions. Variable appearance, often non-specific.
  • Appearance (CNS - Cryptococcal Meningitis): Headache, fever, stiff neck, altered mental status. Most common Cause of death .
  • Appearance (Pulmonary): Cough, chest pain, dyspnea, often milder symptoms than other pneumonias.
  • Appearance (Disseminated): CNS, lungs, skin, bone, prostate, liver.
  • Distribution: Worldwide (Cryptococcus neoformans), Pacific Northwest (Cryptococcus gattii).
  • Risk Factors: HIV/AIDS Immunocompromise (HIV/AIDS - classic risk factor, solid organ transplant, corticosteroids), lymphoma, sarcoidosis and pregnancy. Cryptococcus gattii can infect immunocompetent hosts.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: double cell wall and thick capsule (â€œhaloâ€ appearance), may have one or more buds (blastoconidia); collections of organisms look like soap bubbles
  • Special Stains: India Ink stain (CSF - rapid detection of capsule), Mucicarmine stain (capsule stains red), Methenamine silver, PAS highlight yeast in tissue.
  • Culture: Yeast form colonies on Sabouraud dextrose agar, Birdseed (Niger seed) agar (Cryptococcus neoformans - brown colonies). CSF, blood, BAL, tissue cultures. Rapid growth.
  • Cryptococcal Antigen (CrAg) Testing: Serum and CSF CrAg (highly sensitive and specific for Cryptococcus neoformans and gattii). Screening test of choice for cryptococcal meningitis in HIV-positive patients. Quantitative titers for diagnosis and monitoring. Latex agglutination assays.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Cryptococcal Meningitis - Induction Therapy): Liposomal Amphotericin B + Flucytosine combination therapy is preferred for initial 2 weeks. Amphotericin B deoxycholate is alternative, but lipid formulation is preferred for CNS penetration and reduced nephrotoxicity.
  • Drug Selection (Cryptococcal Meningitis - Consolidation Therapy): High-dose Oral Fluconazole step-down therapy after induction, typically 8 weeks.
  • Drug Selection (Cryptococcal Meningitis - Maintenance Therapy): Low-dose Oral Fluconazole lifelong maintenance therapy in HIV/AIDS patients after consolidation to prevent recurrence. Discontinuation possible in stable, ART-treated HIV patients meeting criteria.
  • Drug Selection (Pulmonary/Extrapulmonary Non-CNS Cryptococcosis): Fluconazole (mild-moderate), Itraconazole (alternative), Amphotericin B (severe).
  • Dosing & Duration: Meningitis induction (Ampho B + Flucytosine) 2 weeks, consolidation (Fluconazole) 8 weeks, maintenance (Fluconazole) lifelong in HIV (if indicated). Pulmonary/extrapulmonary duration depends on severity, typically weeks to months.
  • Treatment Modifications: HIV-associated cryptococcosis - ART initiation timing crucial to avoid immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS). Therapeutic lumbar punctures to reduce CSF pressure in meningitis.

Specific Fungi:

Cryptococcus neoformans, Cryptococcus gattii complex.

Unique Identifiers:

Encapsulated yeast, India ink stain, CrAg test, CNS involvement (meningitis), HIV/AIDS classic association, Liposomal Amphotericin B + Flucytosine induction for meningitis, lifelong Fluconazole maintenance in HIV. Differentiate from bacterial meningitis, other fungal meningitis.

Phaeohyphomycosis

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Subcutaneous): Solitary subcutaneous nodule or cyst. Slowly progressive. Often on extremities, trauma history. Can ulcerate, drain.
  • Appearance (Systemic/CNS): Rare dissemination, CNS tropism especially with Cladophialophora bantiana. Brain abscess. Immunocompromised hosts.
  • Distribution: Subcutaneous tissue (extremities, trauma sites), skin (hands, feet, nails), CNS (disseminated).
  • Risk Factors: Trauma (subcutaneous), contact with soil/plants, Cladophialophora bantiana - neurotropic, even in relatively immunocompetent hosts. Immunocompromise increases risk of disseminated disease.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Pigmented (dematiaceous) septate hyphae in tissue (biopsy, cyst aspirate, skin scraping - depending on form). No sclerotic bodies or grains (vs. Chromoblasto and Mycetoma). Cyst-like lesions with fibrous capsule may be seen.
  • Special Stains: Fontana-Masson stain is used to reveal melanin pigmented hyphae. H&E, PAS, Methenamine silver also visualize fungi.
  • Culture: Dematiaceous (pigmented) mold colonies on Sabouraud dextrose agar. Variable growth rates depending on species. Exophiala jeanselmei, Exophiala dermatitidis, Alternaria, Phialophora, Curvularia, Bipolaris, Cladophialophora bantiana are examples.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Subcutaneous): Surgical excision often curative for localized lesions. Itraconazole, Posaconazole, Voriconazole (oral azoles - adjunct to surgery or for non-surgical candidates, less consistently effective monotherapy). Amphotericin B (lipid formulation for severe/disseminated).
  • Drug Selection (CNS Phaeohyphomycosis - Cladophialophora bantiana): Amphotericin B + Flucytosine combination. Voriconazole or Posaconazole. Aggressive surgical excision/debridement if feasible is crucial. Prognosis poor.
  • Drug Selection (Cutaneous/Superficial): Topical antifungals (azoles, allylamines) for tinea-like forms (N. hyalinum/dimidiatum). Not typically needed for classic subcutaneous cysts/nodules after excision.
  • Dosing & Duration: Subcutaneous - Oral azoles often prolonged (months) even after excision, depending on species and extent. CNS - prolonged IV and oral therapy, duration depends on clinical response, often very prolonged.

Specific Fungi:

Large and diverse group of dematiaceous fungi. Exophiala jeanselmei, Exophiala dermatitidis (Wangiella dermatitidis), Alternaria, Phialophora, Curvularia, Bipolaris, Cladophialophora bantiana (neurotropic), Fonsecaea (rare cause, more common in chromoblastomycosis).

Unique Identifiers:

Pigmented (dematiaceous) hyphae in tissue, subcutaneous cysts/nodules, trauma history, variable clinical forms, surgical excision key for subcutaneous type, poor prognosis for CNS infection, Itraconazole/Posaconazole/Voriconazole for medical therapy. Differentiate from chromoblastomycosis (sclerotic bodies), mycetoma (grains), sporotrichosis (lymphocutaneous).

Hyalohyphomycosis (Fusariosis, Paecilomycosis)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Cutaneous - Disseminated): Necrotic papules and nodules, often with eschars. Target lesions, ecthyma gangrenosum-like lesions, cellulitis. Angioinvasive, embolic lesions.
  • Appearance (Onychomycosis - Fusarium): Superficial White Onychomycosis, distal subungual. May be rapidly progressive.
  • Appearance (Pulmonary): Invasive pulmonary aspergillosis-like pneumonia.
  • Appearance (Disseminated): Skin, lungs, sinuses, eyes, disseminated, especially in severely neutropenic patients (hematologic malignancy, stem cell transplant).
  • Distribution: Ubiquitous in environment (soil, air, water), skin (disseminated lesions), nails (onychomycosis).
  • Risk Factors: Neutropenia Severe Neutropenia (major risk factor, chemotherapy, stem cell transplant), immunocompromise, burns, catheter-related infections (Fusarium), contact lens keratitis (Fusarium).

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyaline (non-pigmented) septate hyphae, similar to Aspergillus but may be more variable in width and septation and less uniform branching. Banana-shaped or canoe-shaped macroconidia may be seen in culture preparations but not typically in tissue.
  • Special Stains: H&E, PAS, Methenamine silver highlight hyaline hyphae.
  • Culture: Rapidly growing, fluffy mold colonies on Sabouraud dextrose agar, variable colony color depending on species - Fusarium spp. often brightly colored (pink, purple, yellow).

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Invasive Fusariosis): Voriconazole preferred. Posaconazole and Isavuconazole alternatives. Amphotericin B (lipid formulation - less effective than voriconazole). Echinocandins not effective.
  • Drug Selection (Localized Cutaneous/Onychomycosis): Topical antifungals for onychomycosis only â€“ limited efficacy. Oral Voriconazole or Posaconazole for localized but refractory cutaneous infections if surgical excision not feasible.
  • Non-Pharmacologic: Neutropenia reversal (key to successful treatment), remove catheters, surgical debridement of localized lesions.
  • Dosing & Duration: Invasive - Voriconazole or Posaconazole until neutrophil recovery and resolution of infection, prolonged therapy often needed.

Specific Fungi:

Fusarium spp. (most common), Paecilomyces spp., Acremonium spp., Penicillium spp. (excluding Talaromyces marneffei - which is addressed separately in the text but technically also Hyalohyphomycosis).

Unique Identifiers:

Opportunistic, hyaline septate hyphae (Aspergillus-like, but variable), necrotic cutaneous lesions, angioinvasion, neutropenia - key risk factor, Voriconazole preferred treatment, brightly colored Fusarium colonies. Differentiate from invasive Aspergillosis (clinically similar, Voriconazole effective for both, but key microscopic features differ and Galactomannan more helpful for Aspergillosis).

Trichosporonosis

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Disseminated Trichosporonosis - Systemic): Fever, sepsis, pneumonia, cutaneous lesions â€“ papulovesicles, purpuric papules, necrotic nodules. Hepatosplenomegaly, renal failure. High mortality in disseminated disease.
  • Appearance (White Piedra): Superficial hair infection, white nodules on hair shaft (already covered in Superficial Mycoses section).
  • Appearance (Cutaneous - Rare, non-specific): Papules, nodules.
  • Appearance (Onychomycosis - Rare): Onychomycosis.
  • Distribution: Disseminated - widespread, lungs, skin, other organs. White Piedra - hair shaft.
  • Risk Factors: Neutropenia Severe immunocompromise (neutropenia, hematologic malignancy, stem cell transplant - key risk for disseminated disease).

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy: Hyaline hyphae, arthroconidia, blastoconidia in tissue (skin, blood, BAL). Similar to yeast but with hyphae.
  • Special Stains: , PAS, Methenamine silver highlight fungi.
  • Culture: Yeast-like colonies on Sabouraud dextrose agar, cream-colored, rapid growth. Urease positive.
  • Identification to species level increasingly important due to variable antifungal susceptibility.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (Disseminated Trichosporonosis): Amphotericin B (lipid formulation) - traditionally first-line, Voriconazole and Posaconazole now considered alternatives or potentially more effective (based on in vitro data, limited clinical data). Echinocandins generally ineffective.
  • Drug Selection (White Piedra): Topical antifungals (ketoconazole shampoo) for hair nodules. Oral Terbinafine may be considered.
  • Dosing & Duration: Disseminated - Prolonged IV therapy with Amphotericin B or Voriconazole/Posaconazole, duration guided by clinical response and source control (if any).
  • Treatment Modifications: Neutropenia reversal key to successful treatment. Catheter removal if catheter-related.

Specific Fungi:

Trichosporon asahii (most common cause of disseminated disease), Trichosporon mucoides, Trichosporon asteroides, and other Trichosporon species. Previously collectively known as Trichosporon beigelii.

Unique Identifiers:

Opportunistic yeast-like fungus (with hyphae and arthroconidia), urease positive, disseminated disease in severely neutropenic patients, White Piedra (superficial hair infection, different clinical context), Amphotericin B/Voriconazole/Posaconazole treatment. Echinocandins generally ineffective. Species identification important due to variable resistance.

Pneumocystis Pneumonia (PCP)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance (Cutaneous - very rare, disseminated PCP): Non-tender papules, nodules, skin-colored or erythematous, may ulcerate. External ear most common cutaneous site.
  • Appearance (Pulmonary Pneumocystis Pneumonia - PCP, primary focus): Progressive dyspnea, dry cough, fever, hypoxia. Bilateral interstitial pneumonia.
  • Distribution: Lungs (primary), disseminated to skin (rare), external ear (rare), other organs in disseminated PCP (rare).
  • Risk Factors: HIV/AIDS HIV/AIDS (classic risk factor, CD4 < 200 cells/ÂµL), organ transplant recipients, corticosteroids, immunosuppression, primary immunodeficiencies.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Microscopy (Lung): Alveolar "foamy" exudate on bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) or induced sputum. Cysts and trophozoites (different life cycle forms, stains differentiate).
  • Microscopy (Skin - very rare): Foamy infiltrate in dermis.
  • Special Stains: GMS stain (cysts - classic "cup-shaped" cysts), Diff-Quik stain (trophozoites), Direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) stain. Giemsa - stains both cysts and trophozoites but less sensitive for cysts.
  • PCR: Highly sensitive PCR assays available on BAL, induced sputum, blood (research settings).
  • Beta-D-Glucan Assay: Serum Beta-D-Glucan - Elevated, very sensitive but non-specific. Helpful to rule out PCP if negative, but positive test requires confirmation by other methods.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Drug Selection (PCP - first-line): Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole (TMP-SMX) (oral or IV).

Causative Organism:

Pneumocystis jirovecii (fungus, not protozoan).

Unique Identifiers:

Alveolar "foamy" exudate, cup-shaped cysts on GMS stain, TMP-SMX treatment, HIV/AIDS classic association, pneumonia, non-specific skin lesions (very rare). Beta-D-Glucan elevated, but not specific. Differentiate from bacterial pneumonia, other opportunistic pneumonias in immunocompromised hosts.

Median Rhomboid Glossitis (MRG)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Sharply demarcated, smooth, erythematous, lozenge-shaped area on the mid-posterior dorsal tongue. Size ranges from <1 cm to nearly 3 cm. Chronic lesions may develop a micronodular surface.
  • Distribution: Mid-posterior dorsal tongue. May have a "kissing lesion" on the posterior hard palate.
  • Risk Factors: Immunosuppression (HIV, diabetes, steroid use), chronic trauma or irritation, use of dentures. Not typically seen in children.
  • Symptoms: Usually asymptomatic. Some patients report mild burning or itching sensation.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Clinical diagnosis: Usually sufficient based on characteristic appearance.
  • Fungal Culture: Positive for Candida albicans.
  • Biopsy (If Needed): Shows chronic inflammatory infiltrate, possible hyperplastic candidiasis in long-standing cases.

Treatment Specifics:

  • Antifungal Therapy (First-Line): Clotrimazole oral troches, 10 mg (dissolve 1 troche in the mouth 5 times daily for 10 days) or Fluconazole 100 mg (Day 1: 2 tablets, Days 2-6: 1 tablet daily).
  • Adjunctive Measures: Improve oral hygiene, avoid smoking, alcohol, and spicy foods. For dentures: disinfect regularly to prevent recurrence.
  • Note: Spontaneous resolution may occur in some cases.

Specific Fungi:

Candida albicans.

Unique Identifiers:

Lozenge-shaped erythematous atrophy on mid-posterior dorsal tongue, loss of filiform papillae, possible "kissing lesion" on posterior hard palate. Differential diagnosis includes oral squamous cell carcinoma, erythroplakia, geographic tongue, granular cell tumor, and vascular lesions.

Angular Cheilitis

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Erythema, cracking, maceration, and fissuring at the corners of the mouth. Bilateral involvement is typical (unlike herpes simplex). Chronic cases may appear thickened, crusted, or eczematous.
  • Distribution: Oral commissures (corners of the mouth). May extend to wider perioral area in lip-licking dermatitis variant.
  • Risk Factors: Poorly fitting dentures, chronic lip-licking, dry/cold climates, diabetes mellitus, immunosuppression, nutritional deficiencies (iron, B vitamins, zinc), inflammatory bowel disease.
  • Symptoms: Mild burning, pain, or discomfort. May be exacerbated by mouth opening, spicy foods, or acidic beverages.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Clinical diagnosis: Usually sufficient.
  • Fungal Culture or KOH Prep: Positive for Candida if fungal infection is suspected.
  • Bacterial Culture: Identifies Staphylococcus aureus in superinfected cases.
  • Blood Tests (if refractory): Iron studies, vitamin B2/B6/B12, folate, zinc levels, diabetes screening.

Treatment Specifics:

  • General Measures: Address underlying risk factors (nutritional deficiencies, diabetes, poor oral hygiene), proper denture hygiene and disinfection.
  • Topical Treatment (First-Line): Antifungal therapy (Nystatin cream, Clotrimazole cream 2-3 times/day), antibacterial therapy if needed (Mupirocin ointment for Staph), anti-inflammatory therapy (mild steroid cream combined with antifungal for severe inflammation).
  • Systemic Treatment (For Refractory Cases): Oral antifungals (Fluconazole 100 mg daily for 7 days) if intraoral candidiasis present, oral antibiotics if extensive bacterial superinfection.

Specific Fungi/Bacteria:

Candida albicans and/or Staphylococcus aureus.

Unique Identifiers:

Bilateral erythema, fissuring, and maceration at oral commissures. Associated with dentures, diabetes, nutritional deficiencies. Treatment includes topical antifungals, mild steroids, and addressing underlying causes. Differential diagnosis includes herpes simplex (unilateral), lip-licking dermatitis, secondary syphilis, and atopic/seborrheic dermatitis.

Erosio Interdigitalis Blastomycetica (EIB)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Round-to-oval erythematous patches & plaques with macerated appearance. Pustules that rupture, leaving an erythematous base with a collarette of scale. Pain and pruritus are common.
  • Distribution: Most commonly affects the third web space of the hands (between middle and ring fingers). Can also involve toe web spaces.
  • Risk Factors: Frequent handwashing (nurses, dishwashers, cleaners), occupational exposure & moisture retention, occlusive footwear, warm/humid climates, diabetes mellitus, immunosuppression, obesity.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Clinical diagnosis: Based on history & examination.
  • KOH Preparation: Reveals budding yeast and pseudohyphae (confirming Candida infection).
  • Fungal Culture: Can identify Candida species if diagnosis is uncertain.
  • Bacterial Culture: If Gram-negative bacilli are present, consider adding antibacterial treatment.

Treatment Specifics:

  • General Measures: Eliminate moisture and irritation, keep web spaces dry (absorbent powders, avoid occlusion), wear protective gloves, remove tight-fitting rings.
  • Topical Treatment (First-Line): Nystatin cream (100,000 units/g), Ketoconazole 2% cream, or Econazole cream applied twice daily until resolution. For bacterial superinfection: Topical gentamicin 0.1% cream for Gram-negative coverage.
  • Systemic Treatment (For Refractory Cases): Fluconazole 100 mg daily for 7-14 days.

Specific Fungi:

Candida species.

Unique Identifiers:

Affects third web space of hand, macerated erythematous plaques with satellite pustules, history of prolonged water immersion. Differential diagnosis includes irritant contact dermatitis, tinea manuum, pustular psoriasis, tinea pedis, erythrasma, and mixed toe web infections.

Candidiasis

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Mucocutaneous Candidiasis:
    • Thrush (Oral Candidiasis): White plaques on tongue, buccal mucosa, palate. Easily scraped off, leaving erythematous base.
    • Vaginal/Vulvovaginal Candidiasis: Thick, white, curd-like vaginal discharge. Pruritus, dysuria, dyspareunia.
    • Perianal & Genital Candidiasis: Erythematous patches with satellite pustules. In men: Candidal balanitis.
  • Cutaneous Candidiasis:
    • Intertrigo: Erythematous plaques in skin folds with vesicles, pustules, satellite lesions.
    • Diaper Dermatitis: Erythematous rash in diaper area with satellite pustules.
    • Other variants: Generalized cutaneous, Candida miliaria, Erosio Interdigitalis Blastomycetica, Candida folliculitis, mammary candidiasis.
  • Risk Factors: Diabetes mellitus, immunosuppression, endocrine disorders, broad-spectrum antibiotics, occlusive clothing, excessive moisture, dentures, indwelling catheters.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Clinical Examination: Erythematous plaques, satellite pustules, white plaques that scrape off easily.
  • KOH Preparation: Budding yeast & pseudohyphae.
  • Fungal Culture: Confirms Candida species.
  • Blood Tests (if systemic suspected): Beta-D-glucan assay.

Treatment Specifics:

  • General Measures: Keep affected areas dry, avoid occlusive clothing, denture disinfection (if oral candidiasis present).
  • Topical Antifungals (First-Line): Nystatin (100,000 units/g), Miconazole 2% cream, Econazole, Ketoconazole creams.
  • Systemic Antifungals (For Extensive Disease): Fluconazole 150 mg single dose (for vaginal candidiasis), Fluconazole 100 mg daily for 7-14 days (for severe cutaneous), Itraconazole 100 mg BID for 14 days (for Candida folliculitis).
  • Special Considerations: Erosio Interdigitalis Blastomycetica needs antifungal + topical antibacterial, treat both mother & infant in mammary candidiasis.

Specific Fungi:

Candida albicans (70-80% of cases), other Candida species.

Unique Identifiers:

Satellite pustules are classic for Candida. Oral candidiasis presents with white plaques that scrape off easily. Check for diabetes or immunosuppression in recurrent cases. Differential diagnosis includes tinea infections, erythrasma, contact dermatitis, seborrheic dermatitis, and herpes simplex.

Granuloma Gluteale (Adultorum & Infantum)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Granuloma Gluteale Adultorum (GGA):
    • Appearance: Flat-topped papules & nodules (often erosive), pseudoverrucous papules, bright or deep red erythematous patches/plaques with maceration.
    • Location: Primarily on buttocks, can involve vulva or areas with occlusive dressing.
    • Symptoms: Severe pain (at rest & with pressure), pruritus may be present.
    • Common cause: Topical benzocaine use (Lanacane, OTC anesthetics).
  • Granuloma Gluteale Infantum (GGI):
    • Appearance: Oval, firm, smooth to slightly scaly papules/nodules (5-40 mm), red-purple to red-brown color.
    • Location: Gluteal surface, groin, upper thighs, lower abdomen. Spares inguinal & gluteal clefts.
    • Common causes: Prolonged irritant dermatitis, topical halogenated corticosteroids, Candida overgrowth.
  • Risk Factors: Chronic moisture, occlusion, topical agents (benzocaine, corticosteroids), Candida superinfection.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Clinical Diagnosis: History of benzocaine use (GGA) or topical corticosteroid use (GGI), distinctive nodular lesions.
  • Biopsy (rarely needed): Nonspecific inflammation, no true granulomas (despite the name).
  • Fungal studies: May identify Candida if superinfection is present.

Treatment Specifics:

  • GGA Treatment: Discontinue benzocaine (manage withdrawal flares with prednisone taper), keep area dry, avoid occlusive dressings, topical high-potency corticosteroids (max 2 weeks), oral corticosteroids (prednisone 0.5â€“1 mg/kg/day tapered over 2-3 weeks), antifungals if Candida present.
  • GGI Treatment: Discontinue halogenated corticosteroids, zinc oxide ointment as protective barrier, super-absorbent diapers, frequent changes, mild cleansers. Lesions spontaneously regress in 3-6 weeks.

Associated Organisms:

Candida albicans (secondary superinfection in many cases).

Unique Identifiers:

Despite the name, not a true granuloma. GGA often caused by topical benzocaine in adults. GGI occurs in infants due to irritant dermatitis + corticosteroids. Candida superinfection common. GGA frequently misdiagnosed as STD. GGI spares gluteal & inguinal clefts.

Chronic Mucocutaneous Candidiasis (CMC)

+

Clinical Presentation:

  • Appearance: Chronic, recurrent Candida infections affecting:
    • Oral mucosa: White plaques on tongue and buccal mucosa (thrush).
    • Nails: Thickened dystrophic nails, painful periungual inflammation.
    • Skin: Scaly erythematous plaques on face, scalp, intertriginous areas, trunk, and extremities. Can develop annular/arcuate plaques.
    • Vaginal area: Pruritic, shiny red plaques with satellite pustules, curd-like discharge.
  • Distribution: Mucosal surfaces, skin, nails.
  • Risk Factors: Immune dysfunction (particularly impaired Th17 cell-mediated immunity), genetic mutations (STAT1, AIRE, IL-17F, IL-17RC), associated endocrine disorders.

Morphology & Diagnostics:

  • Clinical Diagnosis: Recurrent Candida infections affecting mucosa, skin, nails that lack response to conventional therapy.
  • KOH Preparation: Budding yeast & pseudohyphae in skin scrapings.
  • Fungal Culture: Identifies Candida species.
  • Skin Biopsy: Shows pseudohyphae & positive PAS stain in stratum corneum.
  • Genetic & Immune Testing: STAT1, IL-17, AIRE mutations, serologic screening for associated endocrinopathies.

Treatment Specifics:

  • General Measures: Maintain skin & oral hygiene, treat underlying immunodeficiency or associated endocrine disorder.
  • First-Line Systemic Antifungals: Fluconazole (preferred) 100-200 mg daily for chronic suppression, Itraconazole/Voriconazole if resistance develops, Posaconazole/Caspofungin/Amphotericin B for severe or refractory cases.
  • Alternative Therapies: Ruxolitinib (JAK Inhibitor) for STAT1 mutation-associated CMC, Granulocyte Colony-Stimulating Factor (G-CSF) to enhance IL-17 production.
  • Monitoring: Annual screening for autoimmune endocrinopathies, long-term antifungal therapy in most cases.

Specific Fungi:

Candida albicans, other Candida species.

Unique Identifiers:

Chronic, recurrent Candida infection due to Th17 dysfunction. Most common genetic mutation: STAT1 gain-of-function. Classic triad: oral thrush, chronic nail dystrophy, & skin plaques resistant to standard therapy. Associated syndromes include APECED (Addisons, hypoparathyroidism, mucocutaneous candidiasis) and Hyper-IgE Syndrome (Job Syndrome).
